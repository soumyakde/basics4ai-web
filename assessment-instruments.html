Chapter 4 · Assessment & Evaluation Framework

Measuring more than task completion

The literature review found that ~70% of K-12 AI education studies rely on self-designed, unvalidated quizzes. Basics4AI's evaluation framework integrates five validated or purpose-built quantitative instruments with two qualitative sources — explicitly organized around the study's six research questions.

Three theoretical commitments

Why this many instruments

Constructivist epistemology

Learning is a process, not a single before/after snapshot — so instruments are administered repeatedly, after every module, not just pre/post.

Multidimensional learning

Following Krathwohl's taxonomy, cognitive, affective, and behavioral dimensions are each covered — conceptual knowledge alone would be an incomplete picture.

Convergent validation logic

Quantitative scores and qualitative narratives capture genuinely distinct facets of the same phenomenon — see Research Methodology.

The five quantitative instruments

Adapted from validated scales, not invented from scratch

RQ1 · Messaging

CCCES — Conceptual Change Cognitive Engagement Scale

Heddy et al. (2018). Measures whether module content is perceived as coherent, plausible, credible, and comprehensible — adapted to a compact 9-item, 4-point scale for ages 10-14.

RQ2 · Individual factors

CCCES — Individual Characteristics subscale

The same instrument's second subscale: attention, personal relevance, and cultural orientation toward each module's content.

RQ3 · Cognitive engagement

SCES — Situational Cognitive Engagement Scale

Rotgans & Schmidt (2011). Task engagement, effort/persistence, and flow — the felt, experiential quality of involvement during each module's activities, distinct from CCCES's evaluation of the content itself.

RQ4 · Motivation

SIMS — Situational Motivation Scale

Guay et al. (2000), grounded in Self-Determination Theory. Four regulatory styles per module: intrinsic motivation, identified regulation, external regulation, amotivation.

Supporting · Pre/post

AI-CI — AI Conceptual Inventory

Zhang et al. (2024) — the first psychometrically validated instrument for middle-schoolers' AI understanding. A 20-item, criterion-referenced measure administered before Module 1 and after Module 7.

Supporting · Pre/post

AI Misconceptions Scale

Author-developed 8 items targeting documented anthropomorphic misconceptions (e.g., "AI has genuine emotions," "AI is universally unbiased") — tracking persistence or reduction across the program.

Qualitative instruments

Two sources, parallel to every quantitative construct

Per-module written reflections

Two prompt clusters at the close of every module: prior knowledge → new idea → how the activity helped (mechanism of learning), and salience → real-world transfer → schema connections → perceived difficulty.

Semi-structured interviews

A 20-minute, six-section post-program protocol with a purposive subsample — from initial motivations through AI misconceptions, module perceptions, personal relevance, and overall program evaluation.

Limitations, acknowledged

Adaptations come with tradeoffs

Reducing Likert points from 5 (or 7, for SIMS) to 4 and eliminating the neutral midpoint improves developmental appropriateness but limits direct comparison to the original instruments' published norms — scores are interpreted in relation to within-program variation, not external benchmarks. Formal confirmatory factor analysis and measurement-invariance testing on the adapted scales, across age, gender, and socioeconomic background, remain a priority for future research beyond this dissertation's current sample size.
