Pre-Pilot Studies

What three early implementations taught the research

Ahead of the IRB-governed pilots at BuffaloPrep and the Town of Amherst, Basics4AI was tested across three very different community settings — a public library, a community center after-school program, and two university-hosted day camps. Insight came from two sources: direct interaction with parents and children, and survey-based responses.

Site 1 · Summer 2025

Audubon Public Library, Amherst, NY

The parents of every attendee were themselves connected to the field of education, which made for constructive dialogue around the program's goals from the start. Interactions with children and parents alike were overwhelmingly positive — even a few passers-by and elders joined in on the modules offered that day.

What worked

Genuinely engaged families, an enthusiastic librarian, and organic participation beyond the enrolled group.

Logistical lessons

The librarian-scheduled format — a 3-hour session once a week for 3 weeks — proved too demanding for children aged 10-14, and the week-long gap between sessions wasn't optimal; parents expressed a preference for more condensed scheduling. The library's requirement that an adult stay for the full session also created real time pressure for families.

Attendance told the same story: 5 children (with parents) on Day 1, dropping to 2 children and their parents the following week — one family mentioned an upcoming vacation as the reason for missing the session after that.

Site 2

Buffalo Community Center, After-School Program

The socioeconomic context here differed meaningfully from Amherst, where parents were mostly UB professors or employees, eager and enthusiastic. The Community Center's Director was equally enthusiastic and personally reached out to roughly 20 parents of 4th–7th graders — but she noted that parents of the younger children generally didn't want them learning about AI unless it was taught through their school system.

Participation

6 participants, grades 6–7, actually attended — a small fraction of the ~20 families contacted. An in-classroom interview was completed by all 7 children who took part across sessions.

Attendance pattern

Parents who did allow participation weren't consistently supportive of full attendance — several picked their children up midway through a session.

Site 3 · February

UB Campus Day Camps

Two one-day, all-day events drew 6 and 7 children respectively. Incentives — a T-shirt for attending the full camp, $25 for participating in the post-session interview — supported completion. As in Amherst, attendees came from a socioeconomically advantaged community (Amherst, Williamsville), and parents were enthusiastic and engaged with their children's participation throughout.

What the pattern shows

Engagement tracked socioeconomic context, not interest in AI itself

Across all three sites, when families participated, the experience was positive — reflections, surveys, and interviews consistently indicated a genuinely positive experience for children. But willingness to commit to a multi-week, recurring program varied sharply by community: families already embedded in an academic/professional environment (Amherst, Williamsville, UB-affiliated) showed higher enthusiasm and follow-through than the Buffalo Community Center families, where AI literacy competed with a belief that AI should be taught "by the school system," not an outside program. This directly shaped the design of the current BuffaloPrep and Town of Amherst pilots — see Pilots & Findings.
