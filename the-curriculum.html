Chapter 3, §3.2–3.3 · The Curriculum

Seven modules, one cognitive arc

Traditional AI education moves technology → algorithm → meaning. Basics4AI inverts it: human reasoning first, then problem context, then AI abstraction.

~7.5 contact hours totalAges 10-14No coding prerequisite
Six design requirements

Every module answers to a literature gap

Developmentally appropriate — scaffolded for exactly this age group, not adapted down from older curricula.

Conceptual-first — meaning before mechanics, understanding before implementation.

Scaffolded progression — concrete experience bridging to abstract AI concepts.

Ethical integration throughout — not a single add-on unit.

Accessible & equitable — minimal technology, low-cost materials.

Assessment beyond coding performance — validated instruments, not task-completion checkboxes.

Content organization

Five conceptual categories

Content is organized around cognitive functions central to both human and artificial intelligence, not around specific technologies: Problem-Solving (Newell & Simon's General Problem Solver, means-end analysis), Reasoning & Decision-Making (rational-agent and task-environment models), Language & Representation (Schank's Script Theory), Constraints & Optimization (Constraint Satisfaction Problems), and Uncertainty & Probabilistic Reasoning.

Module summaries
Module 1

Understanding Natural & Artificial Agents

Paired mouse-vs-robot maze videos let learners compare biological and rule-based intelligence before either is named — closing with an AI-vs-non-AI classification exercise and the curriculum's first ethics seed.

Module 2

Goal-Based Problem Solving

A single, reused building-block task carries learners through means-end analysis, decomposition, and a hands-on supervised-vs-unsupervised learning contrast.

Module 3

Algorithms, Rules & Adaptive Problem-Solving

Tic-Tac-Toe and the Rubik's Cube make algorithmic reasoning, Minimax, and depth-first search observable and strategic, not abstract.

Module 4

Natural Language, Inference & Bias

Grounded in familiar ELA skills — inference, evidence, pronoun resolution — learners compare their own reasoning against LLM output and test for bias directly.

Module 5

Constraint Satisfaction & Optimization

Sudoku, the canonical age-appropriate Constraint Satisfaction Problem, pairs with a simulated shopping task to transfer reasoning into an authentic, ill-structured decision.

Module 6

Uncertainty & Probabilistic Reasoning

From transparent-bag ball draws to weather forecasting, learners build toward interpreting real AI predictions under genuine uncertainty.

Module 7

Capstone — From Understanding AI to Building AI

Learners collect data, train an image classifier, and build a Rock-Paper-Scissors AI in Scratch — synthesizing agents, planning, algorithms, bias, constraints, and uncertainty into one artifact.

Standards alignment

AI4K12 Five Big Ideas & CSTA

Basics4AI is explicitly cross-walked against the AI4K12 Initiative's Five Big Ideas (Perception; Representation & Reasoning; Learning; Natural Interaction; Societal Impact) and CSTA's K-12 Computer Science Standards. Module 7, the capstone, is the only module that substantively touches all five Big Ideas at once — see the Self-Directed Learning page for the full, activity-level alignment audit (which corrects several tags in this table).
