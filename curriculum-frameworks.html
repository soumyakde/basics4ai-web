Chapter 3, §3.4–3.7 · Curriculum Design Frameworks

How the theory becomes a curriculum

Two structural questions sit behind every module: why is this developmentally sound, and how was it actually built? This page covers the constructivist foundations, the two complementary design frameworks, the pedagogical approach, and the facilitator model built to sustain it all.

Constructivist foundations

Piaget and Vygotsky, side by side

Piagetian constructivism

Explains why ages 10-14 are ready: learners are progressively constructing more abstract mental models through interaction and experience, transitioning from concrete to formal operational reasoning. Basics4AI begins with familiar cognitive activities — observation, classification, planning — rather than programming syntax or mathematical formulae.

Vygotskian social constructivism

Explains how instruction should be structured: through the Zone of Proximal Development, scaffolded games, collaborative activities, and guided inquiry that let learners engage with complex AI ideas before internalizing more abstract reasoning — with support gradually withdrawn as understanding takes hold.

Prior knowledge activation, scaffolded progression, and transfer of learning — the three design principles this synthesis produces.

Two complementary frameworks

ADDIE answers "how was it built." UCIT answers "how was learning designed."

Structural framework

ADDIE

Analysis → Design → Development → Implementation → Evaluation. The systematic, iterative process through which the curriculum was actually developed and refined across pilot implementations — informed by the Chapter 2 literature gaps, then iteratively revised based on facilitator feedback and observed learner engagement.

Cognitive framework

UCIT — Universal Constructive Instructional Theory

The cognitive mechanisms through which learning happens: prior-knowledge activation, scaffolded progression from concrete to abstract, knowledge integration and transfer across modules, and embedded reflection. UCIT is what makes the "concrete-before-abstract" pattern (see Games & Activities) a deliberate design choice, not an accident.

Pedagogical operationalization

Problem-Based Learning + Game-Based Learning

Problem-Based Learning (PBL)

Positions authentic, ill-structured problems at the center of learning rather than as an application of previously-delivered content. The capstone Rock-Paper-Scissors AI project (Module 7) is the clearest example: designing, training, evaluating, and improving a system requires integrating everything learned, in service of a genuine problem.

Game-Based Learning (GBL)

Supplies the motivational and experiential foundation — the inherent appeal of play, challenge, and immediate feedback. Games serve as the primary entry point for most concepts: learners encounter ideas through play before connecting them to formal AI terminology, with reflection and discussion built in after each activity.

Facilitator Support Framework

Designed for scale, from the start

Basics4AI is designed to be delivered by volunteer facilitators — not AI or computer-science experts — reducing dependence on highly specialized instructors and increasing scalability. Two dedicated resources, described in full on their own pages, operationalize this:

Facilitators

Professional Development Program →

A 2-day intensive workshop grounded in Desimone's PD framework, TPACK, Gradual Release of Responsibility, and Universal Design for Learning — full 4-phase program detailed on its own page.

 Facilitators

Self-Directed Learning Resources →

A 5-tier, Bloom's-aligned progression per module — Getting Started through Expert Level — extending learning beyond the formal instructional session.
