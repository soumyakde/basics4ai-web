Appendix A · Games & Activities

Why this game, for this concept

Every activity in Basics4AI was chosen for a reason — not just what to do, but what AI or cognitive concept it was designed to make visible, and how it contributes to that module's learning objectives.

Full kit cost: under $20 per child
Per-module highlights

Selected activities and their rationale

Module 1

Mouse-vs-robot maze videos

A biological agent (trial-and-error, memory, sensory exploration) paired directly against an artificial one (fixed wall-following rule) — letting learners observe the difference between adaptive and rule-based intelligence before either is formally defined.

Module 2

Supervised vs. unsupervised building

The same building-block task, first with labeled reference images, then without — isolating exactly what labels and guidance contribute to learning, using identical materials for a clean contrast.

Module 3

Playing against an AI opponent

Escalating skill levels, including an unbeatable version, manufacture the module's central cognitive conflict: the AI plays well without "thinking" the way a human does — the engine of the anti-anthropomorphism goal running through the whole curriculum.

Module 4

Comparing inferences with Claude, Gemini, ChatGPT

Learners perform the same evidence-based inferencing task as contemporary LLMs, then compare outputs — positioning AI output as something to evaluate, not trust by default.

Module 5

Sudoku as the CSP anchor

Every cell a variable, every row/column/box a constraint — selected as the most natural, age-appropriate embodiment of a classical Constraint Satisfaction Problem, paired with a simulated shopping task for real-world transfer.

Module 6

Transparent vs. opaque bag draws

Isolates information-visibility as the sole source of uncertainty — the same materials, hidden or not, show learners exactly where unpredictability comes from.

Module 7

Rock-Paper-Scissors capstone

Universally known rules, three visually distinct classes — ideal for an image-classification task that gives the trained model a functioning, competitive context rather than an abstract demo.

Cross-cutting

The human-vs-machine comparison, three times

Mouse vs. robot (M1), human vs. AI Tic-Tac-Toe (M3), human vs. robot Sudoku (M5) — the same comparison structure reused at increasing sophistication to durably challenge the belief that AI "thinks" like a person.

Cross-module design patterns

Six recurring patterns tie the whole curriculum together

PatternInstructional function
Concrete-before-abstract progressionReduces cognitive load by grounding new AI vocabulary in direct experience before it's named.
Human-vs-machine comparison, repeated 3×Durably challenges the belief that AI "thinks" like a person.
Scaffold withdrawalGradually transfers responsibility from facilitator to learner within each module.
Ethics integrated throughoutContinuous rather than an add-on — addressing the gap where only 15% of reviewed studies included ethics.
Formal activity + everyday-life transferEach module pairs a structured activity with a recognizable real-world analog, supporting personal relevance (RQ2).
Explicit cross-module callbacksThe same concept (e.g., self-driving cars, optimization, bias) resurfaces at increasing complexity — a spiral curriculum.
Materials & cost

Under $20 per child

The full kit — mazes, building blocks, a Tic-Tac-Toe board, a Rubik's Cube, Sudoku puzzles, dice, colored balls — is deliberately inexpensive and widely available, keeping the barrier to adoption low for community programs, libraries, and schools without dedicated technology budgets.
