## Research Background *(Chapter 1)*

- **The social pervasiveness of AI** — WEF's 85%-of-2030-jobs stat, AI as "black box," anthropomorphism and misinformation risk for young learners, digital-divide concern
- **AI in education today** — personalized learning, automated assessment, and the flip side: surveillance/predictive systems and equity risk
- **Why current AI-ed is unprepared** — coding-first bias in existing curricula, disconnect between technical complexity and developmental readiness
- **The developmental case for ages 10-14** — Piaget's concrete-to-formal-operational transition + Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development, why this specific age window is the "sweet spot" for scaffolded AI reasoning
- **Statement of purpose** — design/implement/evaluate Basics4AI as a cognitive- and contextually grounded AI literacy framework
- **The six research questions** — RQ1 (messaging perception) through RQ6 (mixed-methods explanation), listed plainly
- **Study context** — IRB Protocol STUDY00009409 ("Mind Meets Machine: A Cognitive Approach to Artificial Intelligence (AI) Basics"), ~90 participants across cohorts, BuffaloPrep, 5 weeks / 10 sessions

## Literature Review *(Chapter 2)*

- **Methodology note** — Systematic Standalone Literature Review of 7 SLRs (2022-23), synthesizing 299 empirical studies; only 25 (≈8%) specifically targeted ages 10-14
- **Key findings, condensed**:
  - 60% of curricula lead with coding, not concepts
  - ~70% of assessment relies on unvalidated, skill-only measures
  - Only 15% of studies include explicit ethics instruction
  - Teachers report low confidence, minimal PD, time constraints
  - Heavy reliance on human-AI analogies ("AI is like a brain") reinforces misconceptions
  - No long-term, multi-session engagement strategies in most existing work
- **The gaps → Basics4AI's response** — a table pairing each literature gap with the specific design decision that addresses it (adapted from the dissertation's Table 2-1)
- **Global policy context** — brief mention of AI4K12, UNESCO, CSTA as the standards landscape Basics4AI is positioned against

## Curriculum Design Frameworks *(Ch. 3, §3.4–3.7)*

- **Constructivist foundations** — Piagetian schema-construction (why 10-14 is developmentally ready) alongside Vygotskian scaffolding/ZPD (how instruction should be structured), presented side-by-side
- **Two complementary design frameworks**:
  - **ADDIE** (Analysis → Design → Development → Implementation → Evaluation) — the *structural* process: how the curriculum was actually built and iteratively refined through pilots
  - **UCIT** (Universal Constructive Instructional Theory) — the *cognitive* framework: prior-knowledge activation, scaffolded progression, knowledge integration/transfer, reflection
  - One-line distinction: "ADDIE answers *how was it built*; UCIT answers *how was learning designed*"
- **Pedagogical operationalization** — Problem-Based Learning (inquiry, authentic problems, the Rock-Paper-Scissors capstone as the exemplar) + Game-Based Learning (motivation, "pleasantly frustrating" challenge, reflection-after-play)
- **Summary table** — educational function → primary framework (Piaget / Vygotsky / UCIT / PBL / GBL), adapted from Table 3.6
- **Facilitator Support Framework** — volunteer-facilitator model (no CS/AI expertise required), the 2-day PD workshop design (Desimone's framework, TPACK, Gradual Release of Responsibility, Universal Design for Learning), the 5-tier Self-Directed Learning progression (Getting Started → Building Up → Thinking Hard → Challenge Mode → Expert Level)
- Explicit note: PD workshop and SDL resources are *designed*, not yet formally evaluated — out of this dissertation's current scope, flagged as future work

## Professional Development (PD) Handbook *(new — `B4AI_PD_v1.pdf`)*

- **What it is** — the design specification for training volunteer, non-specialist community facilitators to deliver Basics4AI at scale across libraries, community centers, and after-school programs
- **Seven converging frameworks** grounding the program: Desimone's 5 features of effective PD, TPACK, Knowles' andragogy, Gradual Release of Responsibility, Wenger's Communities of Practice, Universal Design for Learning, and AI4K12/CSTA standards alignment
- **The 4-phase model** (6–8 weeks per cohort) — the page's central visual:
  1. **LEARN IT** — ~8 hrs asynchronous self-study, experiencing all 7 modules as a *student* first
  2. **TEACH IT** — 2-day, 16-hour in-person intensive (Day 1: content + pedagogy; Day 2: microteaching + peer feedback)
  3. **DO IT** — 2–4 real co-facilitated deliveries with structured observation and coaching
  4. **SUSTAIN IT** — ongoing monthly community-of-practice check-ins, shared adaptations library, annual refresh
- **Certification pathway** — 4 assessment checkpoints (pre-workshop reflection → microteaching peer scores → first co-facilitation observation → independent delivery self-assessment), each with explicit rubric thresholds; certification doesn't expire but requires an annual refresh
- **Content knowledge domains facilitators must develop** — AI concepts, algorithms & problem solving, probability & uncertainty, NLP, ML engineering, AI ethics — condensed as a short list, not the full table
- **Child safety section** — background checks, the two-adult rule, code of conduct, mandated-reporter orientation — worth a brief, serious mention since this reflects real program rigor around working with minors
- **The 10-document deliverables map** — presented as a simple list (Program Design Doc, Facilitator Handbook, Quick-Reference Cards, Observation Rubric, Readiness Self-Assessment, Microteaching Protocol, Workshop Slides, Troubleshooting Guide, Community of Practice Starter Kit, Participant Feedback Form) to convey the program's completeness without reproducing every document
- **AI4K12/CSTA alignment table** — condensed version of the same module-by-module standards mapping, framed for facilitators ("why are we learning this" in one sentence per module)
