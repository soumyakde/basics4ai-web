Chapter 2 · Literature Review

Mapping the gaps in K-12 AI education

Rather than re-synthesizing hundreds of individual studies, this review takes a systematic-review-of-systematic-reviews approach — analyzing 7 SLRs that together already synthesize 299 empirical studies.

7 SLRs reviewed 299 studies synthesized Only 25 (~8%) targeted ages 10-14
Methodology

A systematic standalone literature review

Following Okoli's (2015) Systematic Standalone Literature Review (SSLR) methodology, the search spanned Google Scholar, ACM Digital Library, and ProQuest, ultimately narrowing 87 initial studies to 7 systematic literature reviews published 2022-23 — the years AI-education research surged from roughly 5 papers/year to 71 in a single year. Reviewing a curated set of existing SLRs, rather than a first-order synthesis of primary studies, was a deliberate choice: the 7 SLRs collectively cover 299 empirical studies, a breadth no single primary review could achieve within a dissertation's scope.

Key findings

Six converging patterns across the literature

Curriculum

60% lead with coding

The overwhelming majority of curricula adopt a "coding-first" approach via Scratch, Blockly, or Python — reinforcing the misconception that AI is "only for coders."

Assessment

~70% skill-only

Assessment remains predominantly skill-oriented (task completion, project rubrics) rather than measuring conceptual understanding, with self-designed quizzes lacking psychometric validation.

Ethics

Only 15% include it

Ethical instruction remains largely absent from existing curricula, and even the 15% that include it tend to address bias and privacy only at a surface level.

Developmental fit

~8% target ages 10-14

Of 299 studies, only 25 specifically examined upper-primary and lower-middle-school learners — the exact population this dissertation addresses.

Teachers

Low confidence, minimal PD

Nearly half of surveyed teachers feel unprepared to explain AI's technical or societal dimensions; AI-specific pedagogy is largely absent from pre-service training.

Analogies

"AI is like a brain"

Heavy reliance on human-AI analogies reinforces anthropomorphic misconceptions rather than building accurate conceptual models.

The response

Gaps in the literature → Basics4AI's design decisions

Gap in current AI educationBasics4AI's response
Coding-first approaches overwhelm young learnersConcept-first design — begins with natural-vs-artificial agent problem-solving through physical games before any digital tool appears.
Overdependence on expensive hardware/softwareUnplugged, minimal-technology design — inexpensive, readily available games; online activities need only free tools and an internet connection.
Lack of rigorous, validated assessmentValidated instrument battery — AI-Conceptual Inventory, CCCES, SCES, SIMS, and an author-developed misconceptions scale (see Research Background).
Minimal ethical instructionEthics in every module — e.g., natural-language concepts (Module 4) directly teach sources of bias and hallucination.
Teachers report low confidenceA dedicated facilitator Professional Development program with self-directed learning support (see PD Handbook).
Not developmentally appropriate for ages 10-14Playful, scaffolded design — physical before digital, simple before complex.
Over-reliance on human-AI analogiesRule-based explanations — "AI follows instructions, like a recipe," not "AI is like a brain."
No long-term engagement strategyA 7-module, 10-session, 5-week scaffolded curriculum — sustained engagement beyond single-session formats.
Global policy context

Situated within a broader movement

Organizations including AI4K12, UNESCO, ISTE, and CSTA are actively working to embed AI literacy within national and international educational standards. Basics4AI adopts its own cognitive- and context-based structure while remaining explicitly aligned with the AI4K12 Five Big Ideas and CSTA K-12 Computer Science Standards — giving the curriculum a recognized vocabulary within this broader conversation (see The Curriculum for the full alignment table).
